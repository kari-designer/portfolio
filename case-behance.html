00Overview

What & why

A personal site for a designer who builds modern websites and mentors others. It had to do three jobs at once — convert visitors into leads, prove skill, and feel premium and a little bold, not like a template.

The hardest constraint: the site itself is the portfolio. Every interaction is a tiny demo of the work — so the quality of the build is the argument.

ProblemChallenge

The problem

Most freelance portfolios look like generic agency templates or hide the work behind clicks. Visitors leave before they get the value, and there's no clear, low-friction way to start a conversation.

GoalsSuccess

Goals

1 — Move a visitor from “who is this?” to “let's talk” in one scroll. 2 — Show skill through the experience, not claims. 3 — Make contacting effortless, landing leads straight in Telegram.

01UX

How the experience is structured, and the reasoning behind every key decision.

Step 01Architecture

One page, not five

A single long page with anchor navigation and smooth scroll instead of separate routes. For a portfolio, every extra click is a chance to leave — one canvas lets me control the narrative like a story.

Decision → one-page, smooth-scroll anchors. Why → fewer drop-off points and a sequenced pitch. Result → the visitor is pulled from hook to CTA without ever “navigating”.

Step 02Order

The narrative order

Hero → About → Skills → Work → Services → Mentoring → Pricing → Testimonials → Contact. It mirrors how trust builds: hook → who → proof → what I do → social proof → price → act. Pricing sits after proof and testimonials on purpose — by then the value is obvious, so the number feels fair.

Step 03Navigation

Cut the menu to 5

The header shows only About · Work · Services · Mentoring · Pricing. It used to have 8 items and felt corporate. Fewer links = clearer hierarchy, less load; a scroll-spy highlights the current section so you never feel lost on a long page.

Step 04The CTA

Why “Discuss a project”, not “Contact”

The primary button reads “Discuss a project”, not “Contact us” or “Submit”. “Contact” is a chore; “discuss a project” is a low-commitment, collaborative invitation that matches how freelance deals actually begin. High-contrast solid fill makes it unmistakably the action.

Discuss a project← the one button that matters
Step 05The form

One field beats two

The form first asked for Email and a separate Telegram/phone field — people hesitated over which to fill. I merged them into a single required field, “How to reach you (email, Telegram or phone)”. Less thinking, more submits. Leads go straight to Telegram via a bot — replies in minutes, not days.

Step 06Details

Bilingual, accessible, fast

Instant UA/EN toggle (Kyiv + worldwide clients), all motion respects prefers-reduced-motion, and heavy effects switch off on mobile so scrolling stays smooth. Hand-coded, no framework — it loads fast.

02UI

The visual language and the system everything is built on.

Step 07Direction

Dark, premium, one bold accent

A near-black canvas with generous whitespace reads expensive and lets the work pop. A single indigo→pink accent carries energy without a rainbow. Big, tight Inter type does the talking; everything else stays quiet.
