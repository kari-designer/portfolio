00Overview

What & why

The brief: a personal site for a designer who builds modern websites and mentors others. It had to do three jobs at once — convert visitors into leads, prove design skill, and feel premium and a little bold, not like a template.

The hardest constraint: the site itself is the portfolio. Every interaction has to be a tiny demo of what the designer can do — so quality of the build is the argument.

ProblemThe challenge

The problem

Most freelance portfolios either look like generic agency templates or hide the work behind clicks. Visitors leave before they understand the value, and there's no clear, low-friction way to start a conversation.

GoalsSuccess

Goals

1 — Get a visitor from “who is this?” to “let's talk” in a single scroll. 2 — Show skill through the experience, not just claims. 3 — Make contacting effortless (and land leads straight in Telegram).

01UX

How the experience is structured, and the reasoning behind every key decision.

Step 01Architecture

One page, not five

I chose a single long page with anchor navigation and smooth scroll instead of separate routes. For a portfolio, every extra click is a chance to leave. One canvas lets me control the narrative order like a story.

Decision → one-page with smooth-scroll anchors. Why → fewer drop-off points, and I can sequence the pitch. Result → the visitor is gently pulled from hook to CTA without ever “navigating”.

Step 02Order

The narrative order

Hero → About → Skills → Work → Services → Mentoring → Pricing → Testimonials → Contact. It mirrors how trust is built: hook → who → proof → what I do → social proof → price → act. Pricing comes after proof and testimonials on purpose — by then the value is obvious, so the number feels fair.

Step 03Navigation

Cut the menu to 5

The header shows only About · Work · Services · Mentoring · Pricing. Earlier it had 8 items and felt like a corporate site. Fewer links = clearer hierarchy and less cognitive load; the rest is reachable by scrolling or the single CTA.

Why → Miller's law & focus. A short, scannable menu reads as confident. A scroll-spy highlights the section you're in, so you never feel lost on a long page.

Step 04The CTA

Why “Discuss a project”, not “Contact”

The primary button reads “Обговорити проєкт” / “Discuss a project”, not “Contact us” or “Submit”. “Contact” is a chore; “discuss a project” is a low-commitment, collaborative invitation that matches how freelance deals actually start. I also gave it a high-contrast solid fill so it's unmistakably the action on the page.

Discuss a project ← the one button that matters
Step 05The form

One field beats two

The contact form first asked for Email and a separate Telegram/phone field — people hesitated over which to fill. I merged them into a single required field, “How to reach you (email, Telegram or phone)”. Less thinking, less friction, more submits. Leads are sent straight to Telegram via a bot, so replies happen in minutes, not days.

Why → reduce form friction. Every removed decision raises completion. Meeting people where they already chat (Telegram) beats a contact form that emails into the void.

Step 06Bilingual

UA / EN in one click

Based in Kyiv, working worldwide — so every string has a Ukrainian and English version, toggled instantly without a reload (the choice is remembered). No duplicate pages, no language-picker dead-ends.

Step 07Accessibility & perf

Fast and considerate

All the heavy motion respects prefers-reduced-motion and turns off for people who opt out. On mobile I disable scroll-parallax, blur filters and the custom cursor so scrolling stays smooth on real phones. The site is hand-coded, no framework — it loads fast.

02UI

The visual language and the system everything is built on — shown live, not as flat images.

Step 08Direction

Dark, premium, one bold accent

A near-black canvas with generous whitespace reads expensive and lets the work pop. A single indigo→pink accent carries energy without turning into a rainbow. Big, tight Inter type does the talking; everything else stays quiet.
